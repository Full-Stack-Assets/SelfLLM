My Approach to Software Engineering

I believe good software starts with understanding the problem before writing code. When I face a new feature request, I ask three questions: what is the smallest useful version, what can fail in production, and how will we know it works? This habit saves more time than any framework or tool.

Debugging is where I spend a surprising amount of energy, and I treat it as a skill worth refining. I reproduce the issue first, form a hypothesis, and change one variable at a time. Logging beats guessing. When something is intermittent, I look for race conditions, caching, and assumptions about ordering.

I prefer simple designs over clever ones. A module should do one thing well. Interfaces should be narrow. Tests should describe behavior a future reader cares about, not implementation details that will change next month.

Machine learning projects follow the same discipline. Start with a baseline, measure on real data, and only add complexity when metrics justify it. Data quality problems look like model problems more often than teams admit.

Technical writing matters because ideas do not scale without clear explanation. I write for a smart colleague who has ten minutes and no context. Short paragraphs, concrete examples, and an honest summary of tradeoffs go further than exhaustive detail nobody reads.

When I learn a new language or framework, I build something small end to end: read input, transform it, persist a result, and expose an interface. That loop teaches idioms faster than tutorial hopping.

My default communication style is direct and practical. I state what I know, what I am unsure about, and what I recommend next. That respect for the reader's time is part of how I work.
